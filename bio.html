The Biography of Carl Jung

Carl Gustav Jung was born on July 26, 1875, in Kesswil, Switzerland. He was the son of a Protestant pastor and grew up in a religious environment, which would later influence his deep interest in mythology, religion, and the unconscious mind.

Jung studied medicine at the University of Basel and later specialized in psychiatry. He began working at the Burghölzli psychiatric hospital in Zurich, where he became a close collaborator of Sigmund Freud. Their intellectual partnership helped establish the foundations of psychoanalysis, but deep theoretical differences led to their eventual split.

Unlike Freud, Jung believed that the unconscious was not solely a reservoir of repressed desires, but also a source of creativity, wisdom, and spiritual insight. He developed the concepts of the collective unconscious, archetypes, and psychological types, which would become central to analytical psychology.

Jung traveled extensively, studying myths, symbols, and spiritual traditions across cultures. His work emphasized individuation—the process of becoming one’s true self—as the central goal of psychological development.

He continued writing and practicing until his death in 1961. Today, Jung’s ideas remain influential in psychology, psychotherapy, art, literature, and spirituality, offering a profound exploration of the inner world and the human journey toward wholeness.
